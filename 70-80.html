Engine Questions and Answers

Nacelle Engine Mounts and Structure

  • What two systems work together to provide a load path from the engine mount pads to the nacelle Engine Mount Structure (EMS)?

    The Engine Vibration Isolator System (EVIS) and the Hydraulic Torque Compensation System (HTCS) work together to provide a load path from the engine mount pads to the nacelle's Engine Mount Structure (EMS).

  • How many front and aft mount pads does the PW150A engine have?

    The PW150A engine has three front mount pads (two side and one top) and two aft mount pads located on the left and right sides of the Intercompressor Case.

  • What types of loads do the front and aft mount pads transmit?

    The front mount pads transmit axial, vertical, and lateral loads, with the front side pads transmitting most of the engine torque loads. The aft mount pads transmit vertical and lateral loads, as well as the remaining engine torque loads.

  • What is the purpose of the Engine Vibration Isolator System (EVIS)?

    The EVIS has a vibration isolator for each engine mount pad.

  • What is the purpose of the Hydraulic Torque Compensation System (HTCS)?

    The HTCS provides high torsional stiffness to react engine torque loads and low translational stiffness to minimize the transmission of vibrations.

  • What components are included in the nacelle Engine Mount Structure (EMS)?

    The EMS includes the front frame (horsecollar), mid-frame, strut attachment fittings, 14 tubular struts, and the lower cowl.

  • What are the two upper rear struts of the EMS attached to?

    The two upper rear struts of the EMS are attached to the upper rear mounting pads of the turbomachinery module.

  • How is isolator drift checked in service?

    Isolator drift is checked by inspecting the visual drift indicator on the forward top vibration isolator.

  • How are bonding straps arranged on the engine mount system?

    A main bonding strap connects the engine to the pylon at the aft mount. A second bonding strap is located on the forward right mount.

  • What happens in the unlikely event of a fire burning out the elastomer pads and aluminum plate in the forward top vibration isolator?

    In the unlikely event of a fire, the metal plates will contact each other, maintaining the load path from the mount pad to the EMS.

  • What materials are the components of the forward top vibration isolator made from?

    The forward top vibration isolator components are made from an aluminum plate, steel cups, and elastomer pads.

  • What loads are reacted by the forward side vibration isolators?

    The forward side vibration isolators react vertical, lateral, and torsional loads.

  • What material is used for the bracket assembly, link, core, and bolts in the forward side vibration isolators?

    The bracket assembly, link, core, and bolts in the forward side vibration isolators are made from steel.

  • What loads are reacted by the aft vibration isolators?

    The aft vibration isolators react vertical, lateral, and torsional loads.

  • How does the arrangement of the link and spherical bearings on the aft vibration isolator help with differential expansion?

    The arrangement of the link and spherical bearings on the aft vibration isolator allows for freedom of movement to accommodate differential expansion and contraction between the engine and the EMS.

  • What materials are the components of the aft vibration isolator made from?

    The components of the aft vibration isolator are made from steel for the mount and link, and bronze for the spherical bearings.

  • What is the nominal pressure of the HTCS at take-off?

    The nominal pressure of the HTCS at take-off is 2000 psi.

Engine Fuel System

  • What is the purpose of the engine fuel system?

    The purpose of the engine fuel system is to provide a metered flow of clean fuel to the engine's combustion chamber.

  • What components does the engine fuel system have?

    The engine fuel system has a regenerative fuel pump, a fuel metering unit (FMU), a flow divider valve, fuel manifolds, and fuel nozzles.

  • Where does the FMU receive fuel from?

    The FMU receives fuel from the regenerative fuel pump.

  • What happens to the fuel after it leaves the regenerative fuel pump?

    After leaving the regenerative fuel pump, the fuel is routed to the FMU.

  • How does the FMU control fuel flow?

    The FMU controls fuel flow by modulating a variable orifice based on FADEC signals.

  • How does the FADEC calculate the correct amount of fuel?

    The FADEC calculates the correct amount of fuel based on engine parameters, such as Np, Nh, and ITT.

  • What is the purpose of the flow divider valve?

    The flow divider valve splits the fuel flow between the primary and secondary manifolds.

  • What happens to the manifolds when the fuel pressure from the FMU is low?

    When the fuel pressure from the FMU is low, a spring-loaded valve closes, shutting off fuel to the manifolds.

  • What happens to the remaining fuel in the manifolds when the engine shuts down?

    When the engine shuts down, the remaining fuel in the manifolds is drained back to the main fuel tank.

  • How do the fuel manifolds deliver fuel?

    The fuel manifolds deliver fuel to the individual fuel nozzles.

  • What are the characteristics of the fuel nozzle adapters?

    The fuel nozzle adapters are non-return valves that prevent fuel from draining back into the manifold after shutdown.

  • What is the function of the fuel nozzle adapters?

    The function of the fuel nozzle adapters is to provide a spray of atomized fuel into the combustion chamber.

  • What is the purpose of the fuel heater?

    The purpose of the fuel heater is to prevent ice formation in the fuel filter.

  • What is the purpose of the fuel filter?

    The purpose of the fuel filter is to remove contaminants from the fuel before it reaches the FMU.

  • What are the fuel tubes made of?

    The fuel tubes are made of stainless steel.

  • What is the purpose of the characterization plug?

    The purpose of the characterization plug is to store engine-specific data for the FADEC.

    The purpose of the ignition system is to ignite the fuel-air mixture in the combustion chamber during engine start.

  • What are the main components of the ignition system?

    The main components of the ignition system are the ignition exciter, ignition cables, and ignition plugs.

  • What does the PW150A ignition system consist of?

    The PW150A ignition system consists of two independent, high-energy ignition exciters and two igniter plugs.

  • How is the ignition exciter powered?

    The ignition exciter is powered by 115 VAC from the Permanent Magnet Alternator (PMA).

  • What voltage does the ignition exciter output?

    The ignition exciter outputs high voltage pulses to the igniter plugs.

  • How is the ignition exciter connected to the igniter plugs?

    The ignition exciter is connected to the igniter plugs by a high-tension ignition cable.

  • Where are the igniter plugs located?

    The igniter plugs are located in the combustion chamber.

  • What is a key safety tip regarding ignition plugs?

    A key safety tip regarding ignition plugs is to avoid touching them while the ignition system is active, as they can deliver a severe electrical shock.

    The ignition system is activated by the FADEC during engine start or when a flameout is detected.

  • What is the function of the Permanent Magnet Alternator (PMA)?

    The PMA provides electrical power to the FADEC and the ignition exciters.

    The ignition exciter cannot be repaired in the field and must be replaced if faulty.

  • What is the high voltage output of the ignition exciter?

    The high voltage output of the ignition exciter is approximately 20,000 volts.

  • What is the purpose of the ignition exciter circuitry?

    The purpose of the ignition exciter circuitry is to generate a high-energy spark to ignite the fuel.

  • What happens if one of the plugs fails?

Engine Start System

  • How is the engine start system powered on?

    The engine start system is powered on when the battery master switch is selected to ON.

  • What is the purpose of the engine start system?

    The purpose of the engine start system is to rotate the engine to a self-sustaining speed.

  • What initial tests does the FADEC perform when powered up?

    When powered up, the FADEC performs a series of self-tests to ensure proper operation.

  • What actions are required to initiate an engine start after the ignition switch is set to NORMAL?

    After the ignition switch is set to NORMAL, the condition lever must be moved to the START FEATHER position and the start switch must be depressed to initiate an engine start.

  • What happens when the Condition Lever is moved out of the "FUEL OFF" position?

    When the Condition Lever is moved out of the "FUEL OFF" position, the fuel shutoff valve opens.

  • When does the FADEC begin metering fuel flow?

    The FADEC begins metering fuel flow when the engine reaches a rotational speed of approximately 10% Nh.

  • What is the FADEC's role in an automatic engine start on the ground after it has been successfully initiated?

    The FADEC controls the fuel flow and ignition timing during an automatic engine start on the ground.

  • How is "light off" of the engine defined?

    "Light off" is defined as the point at which the engine's combustion chamber has successfully ignited the fuel-air mixture.

  • What happens if an engine does not "light off" within 8 seconds of fuel flow starting?

    If an engine does not "light off" within 8 seconds of fuel flow starting, the FADEC will automatically abort the start.

  • What conditions will cause the FADEC to automatically abort a start on the ground?

    The FADEC will automatically abort a start on the ground if: no "light off" is detected within 8 seconds, the Inter-Turbine Temperature (ITT) exceeds a certain limit, or a hot start is detected.

  • What are the key differences between an in-flight start and a ground start?

    The key differences are that during an in-flight start, there is no starter/generator and the engine is windmilled to a self-sustaining speed, and both igniters are activated.

  • How is Flight Mode distinguished from Ground Mode for the purpose of starting?

    Flight Mode is distinguished from Ground Mode by the PLA being at the flight idle gate.

  • How is the ignition system powered and activated?

    The ignition system is powered by the Permanent Magnet Alternator (PMA) and is activated by the FADEC.

  • What happens if there is a loss of communication from the Engine Cockpit Interface Unit (ECIU)?

    If there is a loss of communication from the ECIU, the FADEC will use a backup mode to control the engine.

  • What two selectable modes of operation does the FADEC ignition system have?

    The two modes are OFF, which disables ignition regardless of ground or flight status, and NORM, which activates ignition during engine starts and commands both ignitors ON during flameout and surge accommodation.

  • How does the Start Select switch on the No. 1 engine remain in the selected position?

    The hold coil magnetically holds the Start Select switch in the No. 1 position until the start sequence is complete.

  • What does the energized time delay relay K3 do?

    The energized time delay relay K3 activates the amber SELECT light and applies voltage to the current limit pin of the GCU, limiting the starting current.

  • What signals the GCU to terminate the output from pin Q, and what happens as a result?

    When the engine reaches 50% Nh, the speed sensor in the starter signals the GCU to terminate the output from pin Q. This removes power from the Start Select switch's hold coil, causing the switch to return to the OFF position.

  • What is the purpose of the 6-second time delay after the start sequence is complete?

    The time delay relay K3 retains current limiting for a further 6 seconds to prevent a high current inrush to the batteries, which may be partially discharged from the engine start.

  • How does the Control System respond to an engine flameout?

    The FADEC can detect a flameout within 1 second and activates the ignition circuitry for both ignitors, canceling it once the engine has recovered.
