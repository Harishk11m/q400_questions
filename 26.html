Fire Protection System Questions

Q1: What are the three primary functions of the fire protection system on the aircraft?

A1: detection, extinguishing and indication of fire conditions.

Q2: How many Advanced Pneumatic Detectors (APDs) are used for fire or overheat detection in the nacelles?

A2: Six APDs are used for fire or overheat detection in the nacelles.

Q3: Where are the smoke detectors located within the aircraft, according to the standard configuration?

A3: Smoke detectors are located with two in the aft baggage compartment, one in the forward baggage compartment, and one in the lavatory.

Q4: Which component uses a single APD for fire or overheat detection?

A4: The Auxiliary Power Unit (APU) uses a single APD for fire or overheat detection.

Q6: What specific fire protection elements are removed as part of the modification mentioned for the forward baggage compartment?

A6: The forward baggage compartment, along with the forward HRD fire bottle, fire or smoke detection, indication, and test functions, are removed as part of the modification.

Q7: How many fire extinguishing bottles are dedicated to the engine nacelles?

A7: Two fire extinguishing bottles are dedicated to the engine nacelles.

Q8: Where are the fire extinguishing bottles for the engine nacelles located?

A8: The fire extinguishing bottles for the engine nacelles are installed in the left wing root.

Q9: What types of fire extinguisher bottles are used for the baggage compartments?

A9: Two High Rate Discharge (HRD) fire extinguisher bottles and one Low Rate Discharge (LRD) fire extinguisher bottle are used for the baggage compartments.

Q10: How is the Low Rate Discharge (LRD) fire extinguisher bottle utilized for the baggage compartments?

A10: The LRD bottle is shared between the FWD and AFT baggage compartments.

Q11: How many fire extinguishing bottles are used for the Auxiliary Power Unit (APU) compartment?

A11: One fire extinguishing bottle is used to suppress fire and overheat conditions in the APU compartment.

Q12: What type of fire extinguisher protects the lavatory waste bin?

A12: The lavatory waste bin is protected by a single thermally-activated fire extinguisher.

Q13: What areas of the aircraft are protected by portable hand-operated fire extinguishers?

A13: The flight compartment and passenger compartment are protected by portable hand-operated fire extinguishers.

Q14: On which panels in the flight compartment are fire or smoke detection events indicated?

A14: Fire or smoke detection events are shown on the Fire Protection Panel (FPP), Caution and Warning Panel (CWP), and Glareshield Panel located in the flight compartment.

Q15: What specific areas of the aircraft have indication and test functions provided on the Fire Protection Panel (FPP)?

A15: Indication and test functions are provided on the FPP for engines, APU, and Forward and Aft Baggage Compartments.

Q16: Where is the indication for lavatory smoke and fire detection provided?

A16: Indication for lavatory smoke and fire detection is provided at the cabin repeater lights.

Q17: Where is the Control Amplifier of the fire protection system located?

A17: The control amplifier of the fire protection system is located on the No. 2 equipment panel.

Q18: What are the primary functions performed by the Control Amplifier?

A18: The primary functions performed by the Control Amplifier are detection and extinguishing monitoring, Built In Tests (BIT) function, controlling advisory lights on the Fire Protection Panel, and controlling warning lights on the Caution and Warning Panel and Glareshield Panel.

Q19: Which advisory lights does the Control Amplifier control?

A19: The Control Amplifier controls the advisory lights on the Fire Protection Panel.

Q20: Besides warning lights, what other audible alert can the Control Amplifier control (if installed)?

A20: Besides warning lights, the Control Amplifier can control the fire bell chime (if optional).

Q21: What type of detectors is used in the nacelle fire detection system to sense fire or overheat conditions?

A21: Advanced Pneumatic Detectors (APD) are used in the nacelle fire detection system to sense fire or overheat conditions.

Q24: What are the three detection zones within each nacelle that are monitored by the APDs?

A24: The three detection zones within each nacelle monitored by the APDs are the Main Wheel Well (MWW) zone, Leading Edge Zone (LEZ), and Propeller Electronic Controller (PEC) area.

Q25: What type of sensor element do Advanced Pneumatic Detectors (APDs) utilize to monitor heating?

A25: Advanced Pneumatic Detectors (APDs) utilize gas-filled sensor tubes to monitor heating.

Q26: What are the two switches located within the APD, and what are their respective functions?

A26: The two switches within the APD are the integrity switch and the alarm switch. The integrity switch provides fault signals, and the alarm switch provides alarm signals.

Q27: What happens if the pressure within the APD's sensor element falls below a minimum rated level? Which indicator illuminates?

A27: If the pressure within the APD's sensor element falls below a minimum rated level, the normally closed integrity switch opens and sends a signal to the control amplifier. The FAULT A or FAULT B light on the FPP comes on.

Q28: What causes the gas within the APD to expand, leading to a fire signal?

A28: A fire or overheat condition will cause gas expansion within the APD, leading to a fire signal.

Q29: To which indicator does the APD directly send a fire signal when the alarm switch closes?

A29: The APD directly sends a fire signal to the appropriate engine PULL FUEL/HYD OFF handle light when the alarm switch closes.

Q30: Besides the PULL FUEL/HYD OFF handle light, what other visual and audible warnings are triggered when the APD's alarm switch closes?

A30: When the APD's alarm switch closes, the following are also triggered: CHECK FIRE DET warning light (flashing red) on the Caution and Warning Panel, Master WARNING PRESS TO RESET light (red) on the Glareshield Panel, ENGINE FIRE PRESS TO RESET light (flashing red) on the left and right side of the Glareshield Panel, and an audible BELL warning (optional).

Q31: What action can the flight crew take to silence the audible BELL warning? What is the subsequent state of the ENGINE FIRE PRESS TO RESET lights?

A31: Pushing either the left or right ENGINE FIRE PRESS TO RESET indicator turns the audible BELL warning off. Both ENGINE FIRE PRESS TO RESET lights remain in steady state for the duration of the alarm condition.

Q32: What gas is used to fill the sensor tubes of the Advanced Pneumatic Detectors (APDs)?

A32: Helium gas is used to fill the sensor tubes of the Advanced Pneumatic Detectors (APDs).

Q33: How does the helium gas within the APD sensor tubes respond to an increase in temperature? What other gas release contributes to pressure increase?

A33: The helium gas is sensitive to changes in temperature; its pressure increases with the increase in temperature. Spot heat releases hydrogen from the central core, which increases pressure in the loop.

Q34: From which electrical buses do the APDs receive their power supply? What is the benefit of this dual power source?

A34: APDs receive electrical power from the 28 Vdc left essential and right essential bus. Malfunction of either bus will not cause complete loss of fire detection in the respective nacelles.

Q35: What happens if an APD sensor tube breaks, leading to a loss of pressure? Which indicator illuminates the Fire Protection Panel (FPP)?

A35: If an APD breaks, the loss of pressure in the sensor will open the integrity switch and turn on the FAULT A or FAULT B light on the FPP.

Q37: How are faults in the APDs located in the engine/LEZ and PEC zones indicated differently from faults in the undercarriage zone APD?

A37: If there is a fault in one of the APDs in the engine/LEZ and PEC zones, a FAULT A indication on the fire protection panel comes on. A fault in the undercarriage zone APD will give a FAULT B indication on the fire protection panel.

Q38: What two indicator lights illuminate simultaneously on the Fire Protection Panel (FPP) when a fault is detected in an APD?

A38: The CHK FIRE DETECT light and FAULT light indications come on at the same time when a fault is detected in an APD.

Q39: How are the sensor loops in the LEZ and PEC connected in relation to the control amplifier? What is the consequence of a PEC loop failure versus an LEZ loop failure?

A39: The sensor loops in LEZ and PEC are connected in series with the control amplifier. If the PEC loop fails, the LEZ loop will still provide fire indications. However, if the LEZ loop fails, the PEC loop will not function as a fire detector.

Q40: What indication might a flight crew receive concurrently with a FAULT A indication?

A40: It is possible to receive a FAULT A indication and an engine fire warning concurrently.

Q41: What is the function of the test switch on the Fire Protection Panel (FPP) for the nacelle fire detection system? What is its operational characteristic?

A41: The test switch on the FPP tests the nacelle fire detection system. It is a two-position, spring-loaded to center, toggle switch.

Q42: During a successful nacelle fire detection system test, what visual indications are presented on the Glareshield Panel?

A42: During a successful nacelle fire detection system test, the Master WARNING PRESS TO RESET light (red) comes on and flashes, and both ENGINE FIRE PRESS TO RESET lights (red) come on and flash on the Glareshield Panel.

Q43: What visual indications are presented on the Caution and Warning Panel and the Fire Protection Panel during a nacelle fire detection system test?

A43: During a nacelle fire detection system test, the CHECK FIRE DET warning light (red) comes on and flashes on the Caution and Warning Panel, and the PULL FUEL/HYD OFF light (red) on the pull-handle of the Fire Protection Panel comes on, along with FAULT A and FAULT B lights (amber) on the Fire Protection Panel.

Q44: What audible warning is triggered during the nacelle fire detection system test (if installed)? What causes the PULL FUEL/HYD OFF handle light to illuminate during a real fire?

A44: The FIRE BELL audible warning comes on (optional) during the test. Any nacelle APD will cause the PULL FUEL/HYD OFF handle to illuminate during a real fire.

Q45: Under what conditions will the ENGINE FIRE PRESS TO RESET advisory lights illuminate during a nacelle fire detection system test?

A45: The ENGINE FIRE PRESS TO RESET advisory lights will come on only if a correct response is obtained from all three nacelle APDs and the control amplifier is functioning normally.

Q46: What action will silence the fire bell during a nacelle fire detection system test or a real fire event?

A46: Pushing either ENGINE FIRE PRESS TO RESET advisory light will silence the fire bell.

Q47: What precaution must be taken during the handling of APDs, and what can result from damage to the sensor tube?

A47: Care must be taken during the installation and removal of the APD. Any damage to the sensor tube can result in a leak of the inert gas, which will result in APD sensor failure.

Q48: How is 28 VDC supplied to the APD for engine number 1?

A48: 28 VDC is supplied from the left essential bus via circuit breaker K7 (FIRE DET ENG 1 IND) through disconnects and configuration connectors to the APD pin C.

Q49: How does the control amplifier determine the integrity of the nacelle fire detection control loops (APDs)?

A49: The control amplifier confirms the integrity of the nacelle fire detection control loops (APD) by the voltage sensed on pins 60 and 65.

Q50: What causes the FAULT "B" advisory light to illuminate on the FPP, according to the "NOTE"?

A50: A loss of voltage on pin 60 causes the control amplifier to illuminate the FAULT "B" advisory light on the FPP.

Q51: What causes the FAULT "A" light to illuminate?

A51: A loss of voltage on pin 65 causes the control amplifier to illuminate the FAULT "A" advisory light.

Q52: How many fire extinguishing bottles are used for the nacelle fire extinguishing system, and where are they located?

A52: Two dual-port fire-extinguishing bottles are used for the nacelle fire extinguishing system, installed FWD and AFT in the left wing root.

Q53: Can both fire extinguishing bottles be used to extinguish a fire in either engine nacelle?

A53: Yes, both bottles can be used to extinguish a fire in either engine.

Q54: What flight deck indications are triggered by a fire or overheat condition in either nacelle?

A54: A fire or overheat condition in either nacelle triggers the PULL FUEL/HYD OFF handle light (for related engine, on the Fire Protection Panel), CHECK FIRE DET warning light (on the Caution and Warning Panel), Master WARNING PRESS TO RESET light on the Glareshield Panel, ENGINE FIRE PRESS TO RESET lights on the left and right side of the Glareshield Panel, and an audible warning bell.

Q55: What action does the flight crew need to take to arm the fire extinguishing bottles for the nacelles? What indication confirms that the bottles are armed?

A55: The flight crew needs to pull the PULL FUEL/HYD OFF handle to arm the FWD and AFT bottles. Two BTL ARM lights on the Fire Protection Panel will come on, confirming that both bottles have continuity on the squib and pressure switches.

Q56: How does the flight crew discharge a fire extinguishing bottle into a nacelle? What indication confirms that a bottle has discharged?

A56: To discharge a bottle, the EXTG switch is set to AFT BTL or FWD BTL position. The BTL LOW light will come on to show the loss of bottle pressure, and the BTL ARM lights go off.

Q57: What zones of the engine nacelle are covered by the fire extinguishing system?

A57: The fire extinguishing system provides extinguisher coverage to the right or left nacelle Primary Engine Zone (PEZ), Leading Edge Zone (LEZ), and Main Wheel Well (MWW) zones.

Q58: What type of Electro-Explosive Devices (EEDs) are used in the fire extinguisher bottles, and what design feature enhances their reliability?

A58: Dual-bridgewire Electro-Explosive Devices (EEDs) are used within each cartridge. Redundant power lines with separate circuit breakers enhance their reliability.

Q59: How is redundancy achieved in the electrical firing circuit for the extinguisher bottles?

A59: Bridgewire "A" of all cartridges is taken from one circuit breaker and routed along the left side, while Bridgewire "B" is taken from a separate circuit breaker and routed along the right side of the aircraft to the respective cartridge. This arrangement gives redundant firing signals.

Q60: What is the purpose of the lanyards on the electrical connectors of aircraft with modification 4Q126352?

A60: The lanyards ensure that the correct connector is connected to the discharge squib during maintenance practices.

Q61: How are the fire extinguisher bottles monitored for pressure during normal operation? What indication alerts the crew to low bottle pressure?

A61: The extinguisher bottles are constantly monitored for pressure during the non-alarm state. The BTL LOW light on the FPP will come on when either bottle has lost pressure.

Q62: How can maintenance personnel determine which bottle has low pressure using the PULL FUEL/HYD OFF handle test?

A62: The PULL FUEL/HYD OFF handle test will show which bottle needs service; the arming light for that bottle will not come on.

Q63: What tool is provided for ground checking the pressure switch operation?

A63: A manual exercise key is supplied for ground checking the proper pressure switch operation.

Q64: What is the primary purpose of the smoke detection system?

A64: The smoke detection system monitors the baggage and lavatory areas for smoke conditions.

Q65: What type of devices are smoke detectors, and how do they function to detect smoke?

A65: Smoke detectors are photosensitive devices that use a light beam that detects smoke particles when the beam is interrupted.

Q66: Where are smoke conditions indicated in the aircraft?

A66: If a smoke or fire condition is sensed, the applicable warning indicator lights in the flight compartment, or the repeater lights in the passenger compartment ceiling come on.

Q67: What components comprise the Smoke Detection System?

A67: The Smoke Detection System has AFT Baggage Smoke Detectors, FWD Baggage Smoke Detector, and Lavatory Smoke Detector.

Q68: What modifications affect the forward baggage compartment smoke detection system, and what specific components are removed as a result?

A68: On aircraft with ModSum 4-458982, 4-458912 (extra capacity) and CR825CH03262 (cargo-combi) incorporated, the forward baggage compartment has been removed along with the smoke detector, forward HRD fire bottle, smoke switchlight and the test switch.

Q69: How are the AFT and FWD baggage smoke detectors monitored, and where do their alarm signals go?

A69: The AFT and FWD baggage smoke detectors are monitored by the control amplifier. Each baggage smoke detector directly turns on the applicable SMOKE advisory light on the Fire Protection Panel (FPP), and the alarm signal is also sent to the control amplifier.

Q70: What indications occur in the cockpit when smoke is detected in the baggage compartment?

A70: When smoke is detected in the baggage compartment, the control amplifier turns on indications on the FPP, Glareshield Panel, and Caution and Warning Panel in the cockpit.

Q71: What indications occur in the passenger compartment when smoke is detected in the lavatory? Is there a cockpit indication for lavatory smoke events?

A71: Smoke in the lavatory compartment will cause the repeater lights in the passenger compartment ceiling to come on, and a single audible warning (high chime) in the passenger address system will sound. There is no cockpit indication of any smoke events in the lavatory.

Q72: What is annunciated when smoke is detected in the aft baggage compartment?

A72: Smoke detection in the aft baggage compartment is annunciated by: SMOKE (red) warning light on the Caution and Warning Panel (flashes), Master WARNING PRESS TO RESET light on the Glareshield Panel (flashes), BAGGAGE AFT SMOKE (red) advisory light on the Fire Protection Panel, AFT FIRE BOTTLE ARM (amber) light on the Fire Protection Panel, VENT VALVE INLET and VENT VALVE OTLT CLOSED (white) lights on the Fire Protection Panel, BAGGAGE AFT EXTG (white) switchlight on the Fire Protection Panel, and three chimes.

Q73: What is the physical location of the two smoke detectors in the aft baggage compartment?

A73: In the AFT baggage compartment, smoke detector 2 is located at the rear and smoke detector 1 is located at the front.

Q74: What automatic action does the control amplifier take when smoke is detected in the AFT baggage compartment, and what indications confirm this action?

A74: When smoke is detected in the AFT baggage compartment, the control amplifier will automatically operate relay contacts to remove electrical power from the AFT baggage vent inlet and vent outlet shutoff valves to minimize airflow. Indication of vent valve status will be shown as VENT VALVE INLT and VENT VALVE OTLT lights on the FPP.

Q75: What is the testing capability of the AFT and FWD baggage smoke detectors?

A75: The AFT and FWD baggage smoke detectors have a Built-In Test capability that completely tests their functionality in response to a test signal from the FPP.

Q76: Where is the lavatory smoke detector installed?

A76: The lavatory smoke detector is installed on the lavatory ceiling.

Q77: What are the indications when the lavatory smoke detector is activated by a smoke condition, specific to Jamco and Kidde smoke detectors?

A77: If activated by smoke, the lavatory smoke detector gives a red alarm indicator light (Jamco) or red status indicator light (Kidde) on the smoke detector, a local audible alarm, and one loud chime alert in the passenger address (P/A) system.

Q78: How can alarms be canceled and the smoke detector reset on aircraft with Jamco smoke detectors? How about with Kidde smoke detectors?

A78: On aircraft with Jamco smoke detectors, pushing the interrupt push-button cancels alarms and resets the smoke detector. On aircraft with Kidde smoke detectors, pushing the horn cancel push-button cancels alarms and resets the smoke detector.

Q79: What are the status indicator conditions for the Kidde smoke detector?

A79: For the Kidde smoke detector, the status indicator conditions are: Off (deenergized/not operational), Steady green (normal operation), Steady red (unserviceable, or alarm condition/smoke detected), Flashes green (serviceable/Type 1 fault detected), and Flashes red (unserviceable/Type 2 fault detected).

Q80: How is the lavatory smoke detector tested?

A80: The lavatory smoke detector is tested by pushing and holding the self-test push-button switch on the detector itself.

Q81: What is the operational characteristic of the AFT baggage compartment test switch?

A81: The AFT baggage compartment test switch is a two-position toggle switch (TEST 1/TEST 2).

Q82: What specific components are tested during TEST 1 and TEST 2 of the AFT baggage compartment test switch?

A82: During TEST 1, smoke detector 1 in the AFT baggage is tested. During TEST 2, smoke detector 2 in the AFT baggage is tested.

Q83: What is annunciated during TEST 1/TEST 2 of the AFT baggage compartment?

A83: During TEST 1/TEST 2, the SMOKE (red) warning light on the Caution and Warning Panel comes on, Master WARNING PRESS TO RESET light on the Glareshield Panel comes on and flashes, BAGGAGE AFT SMOKE (red) advisory light on the Fire Protection Panel comes on, AFT FIRE BOTTLE ARM (amber) light on the Fire Protection Panel comes on, VENT VALVE INLT and VENT VALVE OTLT CLOSED (white) lights on the Fire Protection Panel come on (for aft baggage only), BAGGAGE AFT EXTG (white) switchlight on the Fire Protection Panel comes on, and three chimes.

Q84: What is the operational characteristic of the FWD baggage compartment test switch?

A84: The FWD baggage compartment test switch is a push button test switch.

Q85: What is annunciated during the FWD baggage compartment test?

A85: During the FWD baggage compartment test, the SMOKE (red) warning light on the Caution and Warning Panel comes on, Master WARNING PRESS TO RESET light on the Glareshield Panel comes on and flashes, BAGGAGE FWD SMOKE (red) advisory light on the Fire Protection Panel comes on, FIRE BOTTLE FWD ARM (amber) light on the Fire Protection Panel comes on, BAGGAGE FWD EXTG (white) switchlight on the Fire Protection Panel comes on, and three chimes.

Q86: What are the indications of the lavatory smoke detector during a self-test?

A86: During a self-test, the lavatory smoke detector will show a red alarm indicator light (Jamco) or red status indicator light (Kidde) on the smoke detector, a local audible alarm, and one loud chime alert in the passenger address (P/A) system.

Q87: What happens when the self-test push-button is released on a Kidde smoke detector after a test?

A87: When the self-test switch is released on a Kidde smoke detector, the audible alarm stops and the cabin indications go out. The red status indicator light on the smoke detector goes green or flashes green.

Q88: What tool is necessary to push the self-test push-button switch and horn cancel switch on the lavatory smoke detector?

A88: To push the self-test push-button switch and horn cancel switch, it is necessary to use a thin tool approximately 3 in. (7.6 cm) long.

Q89: From which electrical buses do the FWD and AFT baggage smoke detectors and the LAV smoke detector receive their power supply?

A89: The FWD baggage smoke detector and AFT baggage smoke detector 2 (Aft) receive 28 VDC from the right essential bus via circuit breaker M5, SMK DET BAG/CGO 2. The AFT baggage smoke detector 1 (Fwd) and the LAV smoke detector receive 28 VDC from the right essential bus via circuit breaker L5, SMK DET LAV/CGO.

Q90: What happens to the appropriate squibs when smoke is detected, and what conditions are required for this to occur?

A90: The smoke detection system will automatically arm the appropriate squibs if the squibs have bridgewire continuity and power.

Q91: How does the smoke detection system reset? What happens to the inlet and outlet valves in the AFT baggage compartment upon reset?

A91: The smoke detection system will reset upon removal of the smoke. However, the inlet and outlet valves in the AFT baggage compartment will remain closed until the appropriate circuit breakers are reset.

Q92: How is the pressure in the AFT HRD bottle and LRD bottle monitored? What conditions must be met for the appropriate ARM light to illuminate when smoke is detected?

A92: Pressure in the AFT HRD bottle and LRD bottle is monitored by the control amplifier. If both the HRD bottle pressure switch shows pressure and the squib shows continuity when smoke is detected, the control amplifier will output a ground to light the appropriate ARM light.

Q93: What is the primary purpose of the baggage compartment fire extinguishing system?

A93: The primary purpose of the baggage compartment fire extinguishing system is to suppress fire and overheat conditions in the forward (FWD) and AFT baggage compartments.

Q94: What types and quantities of fire extinguisher bottles are used for the forward and aft baggage compartments in the standard configuration? How are the HRD and LRD bottles distributed?

A94: Two High Rate Discharge (HRD) fire extinguisher bottles and one Low Rate Discharge (LRD) fire extinguisher bottle are used. Each baggage compartment has one HRD fire extinguisher, and the LRD fire extinguisher is shared between the baggage compartments.

Q95: How many cartridges does each HRD fire extinguisher bottle have? How many does the LRD fire extinguisher bottle have? What do these cartridges contain?

A95: Each HRD fire extinguisher bottle has one High Rate fire extinguisher cartridge. The LRD fire extinguisher bottle has two Low Rate fire extinguisher cartridges. Each cartridge contains an Electro-Explosive Device (EED).

Q96: What modification affects the forward baggage compartment fire extinguishing system on aircraft with ModSum 4-458982 and 4-458912? What specific components are removed?

A96: On aircraft with ModSum 4-458982 and ModSum 4-458912 incorporated, the forward baggage compartment has been removed along with the forward HRD fire extinguisher bottle and cartridge.

Q97: How are fire and overheat conditions in the baggage compartments monitored? How is the extinguisher bottle pressure monitored?

A97: Indications on the Fire Protection Panel (FPP) are used to monitor fire and overheat conditions in the FWD and AFT baggage compartments. Extinguisher bottle pressure is also monitored from the FPP.

Q98: What pilot action is required to activate the High Rate fire extinguishers in the baggage compartments?

A98: Pilot action, pressing the applicable SMOKE EXTG switchlight, is required to activate the High Rate fire extinguishers.

Q99: What is the sequence of discharge if a fire is detected in the AFT baggage compartment? What is the purpose of the seven-minute delay?

A99: If a fire is sensed in the AFT baggage compartment, the aft high rate fire bottle will discharge first, followed by the low rate fire extinguisher bottle which discharges automatically after seven minutes. The seven minutes delay is to make sure that any remaining fire will be extinguished.

Q100: What is the sequence of discharge if a fire is detected in the FWD baggage compartment in a standard configuration aircraft?

A100: If a fire is sensed in the FWD baggage compartment, both the high rate and low rate fire extinguishers will discharge at the same time.

Q101: What are the indications on the Fire Protection Panel (FPP) after the High Rate Discharge (HRD) extinguisher is activated for an aft baggage fire? What does each indication signify?

A101: For an aft baggage fire, the FIRE BOTTLE AFT ARM segment on the FPP goes off (blank), showing the HRD EED is fired. The FIRE BOTTLE AFT LOW segment on the FPP comes on (amber), showing the HRD fire bottle is empty (low pressure).

Q102: How long after pushing the EXTG switch during an aft baggage fire does the FIRE BOTTLE FWD LOW segment illuminate, and what does this indicate?

A102: The FIRE BOTTLE FWD LOW segment on the Fire Protection Panel comes on (amber) 22 minutes after pushing the EXTG switch, showing that the LRD fire bottle is empty (low pressure).

Q103: What are the indications on the Fire Protection Panel (FPP) after the HRD extinguisher is activated for a forward baggage fire? What does each indication signify?

A103: For a forward baggage fire, the FIRE BOTTLE FWD ARM segment on the FPP goes off (blank), showing the HRD EED is fired. The FIRE BOTTLE FWD LOW segment on the FPP comes on (amber), showing the HRD fire bottle is empty (low pressure).

Q104: How long after pushing the EXTG switch during a forward baggage fire does the FIRE BOTTLE AFT LOW segment illuminate, and what does this indicate?

A104: The FIRE BOTTLE AFT LOW segment on the Fire Protection Panel comes on (amber) 15 minutes after pushing the EXTG switch, showing that the LRD fire bottle is empty (low pressure).

Q105: What type and amount of fire extinguishing agent does the Aft High Rate Fire Extinguisher Bottle contain, and what is its pressurization? Where is it located?

A105: The Aft High Rate Fire Extinguisher Bottle is a stainless steel container filled with 11 lb. (4.99 kg) of liquid fire extinguishing agent Halon 1301 and pressurized with Nitrogen gas at 360 psig (2482 kPag). It is installed in the aft equipment bay.

Q106: What type and amount of fire extinguishing agent does the Low Rate Fire Extinguisher Bottle contain, and what is its pressurization? Where is it located, and which compartments does it serve?

A106: The Low Rate Fire Extinguisher is a stainless steel container filled with 17.5 lb. (7.94 kg) of liquid fire extinguishing agent Halon 1301 and pressurized with Nitrogen gas at 360 psig (2482 kPag). It is installed in the aft equipment bay, but is shared between the FWD and AFT baggage compartments.

Q107: How does the Low Rate Fire Extinguisher discharge its agent in case of an aft baggage fire? What concentration and duration are maintained?

A107: When a smoke condition is sensed in the AFT baggage compartment and the aft High-Rate fire bottle is activated, the Low-Rate fire extinguisher will automatically discharge after seven minutes. A metering device controls the slow release, letting the bottle discharge the extinguishing agent into the baggage compartment at a concentration of 3% for 45 minutes.

Q108: What happens to the aft baggage compartment inlet and outlet vent valves when a smoke condition is sensed? What indication is provided?

A108: When a smoke condition is sensed in the AFT baggage compartment, the AFT inlet and outlet vent valves will close automatically and give an indication on the FPP.

Q109: On aircraft with modification 4Q126352, what is the purpose of the lanyards attached to the electrical connectors?

A109: The lanyards ensure that the correct connector is connected to the discharge squib during maintenance practices.

Q110: On aircraft with ModSum 4-124265 and 4-190605, what modification is made to the forward discharge tube?

A110: On aircraft with ModSum 4-124265 and 4-190605 incorporated, the discharge tube is removed from the forward discharge head and blanked with a plug.

Q111: On aircraft with ModSum 4-124541, what modification is made to the forward discharge head?

A111: On aircraft with ModSum 4-124541 incorporated, the forward discharge head is blanked with a plug.

Q112: On aircraft with ModSum 4-459188, what component is connected to the low rate fire extinguisher bottles? What is its function, and what maintenance is required after a bottle discharge?

A112: On aircraft with ModSum 4-459188 incorporated, the low rate fire extinguisher bottles are connected with the Dryer Metering Unit (DMU). The DMU removes moisture from the extinguisher agent to prevent freezing. It is a one-time use desiccant unit that must be replaced when a low rate fire extinguisher bottle is discharged.

Q113: Where is the FWD High Rate Fire Extinguisher Bottle located?

A113: The FWD High Rate Fire Extinguisher Bottle is located on the forward baggage compartment aft wall.

Q114: What type and amount of fire extinguishing agent does the FWD High Rate Fire Extinguisher Bottle contain, and what is its pressurization?

A114: The FWD High Rate Fire Extinguisher Bottle is filled with 4.33 lb (1.96 kg) of liquid fire extinguishing agent Halon 1301 and pressurized with Nitrogen gas at 360 psig (2482 kPag).

Q115: If smoke is detected in the FWD baggage compartment, what indications are given, and what is the discharge sequence of the fire extinguishers?

A115: If smoke is detected in the FWD baggage compartment, an indication is given on the FPP. Pilot action is required to manually activate the high rate fire extinguisher, and the low rate fire extinguisher will discharge simultaneously with it.

Q116: Similar to the aft system, what is the purpose of the lanyards on the electrical connectors of the forward system on aircraft with modification 4Q126352?

A116: The lanyards ensure that the correct connector is connected to the discharge squib during maintenance practices.

Q117: What is the function of the High Rate Fire Extinguisher Cartridges? How many are there per High Rate bottle, and where are they located?

A117: High Rate Fire Extinguisher Cartridges cause the extinguisher bottle to discharge its contents through the distribution tubes. There is one cartridge for each High Rate fire extinguisher, attached to its related discharge head on the bottom of the fire extinguisher bottle.

Q118: What type of device is contained within each High Rate fire extinguisher cartridge? How is redundancy achieved in their power supply?

A118: Each cartridge contains a dual bridgewire Electro-Explosive Device (EED). Redundancy is achieved with redundant power lines and separate circuit breakers for mission reliability. Bridgewire "A" is from one circuit breaker on the left side, and "B" is from a separate circuit breaker on the right side.

Q119: How does the activation signal cause the High Rate fire extinguisher bottles to discharge?

A119: When an electrical signal is sent through the bridgewires, the EED ignites and explodes, rupturing a "burst" disk. This causes the extinguisher bottle to discharge its contents through the distribution tubes.

Q120: How many low rate fire extinguisher cartridges are there in the standard configuration, and where are they located?

A120: There are two low rate fire extinguisher cartridges, each attached to its related discharge head, located on the bottom of the LRD fire extinguisher bottle.

Q121: What modifications affect the number of low rate fire extinguisher cartridges and their location?

A121: On aircraft with ModSums 4-458927, 4-124541, and 4-190605 incorporated, there is only one low rate fire extinguisher cartridge, installed at the aft discharge head for the aft baggage compartment. The forward discharge head is blanked with a plug.

Q122: When the extinguisher switch is pushed for an aft baggage fire, what voltage is supplied to the control amplifier, and what is the outcome regarding the HRD bottle pressure and indication lights?

A122: When the extinguisher switch is pushed, 28 VDC is supplied to the control amplifier pins 15, 16, 32, and 33. The control amplifier outputs 28 VDC to the AFT HRD bottle on 2 separate lines, and the cartridge fires. The HRD bottle pressure goes low, the armed light extinguishes, and the BTL LOW light illuminates.

Q123: How long after the extinguish switch is pushed for an aft baggage fire does the LRD bottle squib receive power, and when does the FWD BTL LOW advisory light illuminate?

A123: Seven minutes after the extinguish switch is pushed, the control amplifier outputs 28 VDC to the AFT cargo squib on the LRD bottle. Due to flow restriction, it takes 15 minutes before the LRD pressure switch will show low pressure. At this time the FWD BTL LOW advisory light will illuminate.

Q124: When the extinguisher switch is pushed for a forward baggage fire, what voltage is supplied to the control amplifier, and what happens to the HRD and LRD bottles and their indication lights?

A124: When the extinguisher switch is pushed, 28 VDC is supplied to the control amplifier pins 15, 16, 32, and 33. The control amplifier outputs 28 VDC to the FWD HRD and the LRD bottles on 2 independent lines, and the cartridges fire. The HRD bottle pressure goes low, therefore, the armed light extinguishes and the BTL LOW light illuminates.

Q125: For a forward baggage fire, how do the HRD and LRD bottles discharge, and when does the AFT BTL LOW advisory light illuminate?

A125: The HRD and LRD bottles fire at the same time. Due to restrictors, the LRD bottle pressure switch will not activate until 15 minutes after squib discharge. At this time the AFT BTL LOW advisory light illuminates.

Q126: What is the primary purpose of the Auxiliary Power Unit (APU) fire detection system?

A126: The APU fire detection system senses fire or overheat conditions in the APU compartment.

Q127: How many Advanced Pneumatic Detectors (APDs) are in the APU compartment, and what do they sense?

A127: One Advanced Pneumatic Detector (APD) is located in the APU compartment, which senses changes in temperature.

Q128: What is the primary component of the APU fire detection system?

A128: The primary component of the APU fire detection system is the APU Compartment Advanced Pneumatic Detector.

Q129: What are the two main parts of the APU APD, and how is it installed?

A129: The APU APD has a main element body and a sensor tube. The main element body is held in place by two P-clamps, and the sensor tube is supported by additional clamps and grommets.

Q130: What type of gas fills the sensor tube of the APU APD, and what is its characteristic?

A130: The sensor tube is filled with an inert gas which is sensitive to changes in temperature.

Q131: From which electrical bus does the APU APD receive power, and what two integral switches does it contain?

A131: The APU APD receives electrical power from the right essential bus. It contains an integrity switch and an alarm switch.

Q132: What happens if the APU APD sensor breaks, causing a loss of pressure? Which light illuminates on the FPP?

A132: If an APU APD breaks, the loss of pressure will open the integrity switch and signal the control amplifier to turn on the FAULT light on the FPP.

Q133: What causes the APU APD alarm switch to close, and what is its normal state?

A133: The alarm switch is normally open and closes when an overheat or fire condition occurs, caused by the pressure increase in the APD.

Q134: To which indicator does the APU APD alarm switch directly signal? How long does this indication remain?

A134: The alarm switch directly signals the FIRE advisory light on the FPP. The FIRE (red) advisory light remains on steady state for the duration of the alarm.

Q135: What happens when a fire or overheat is sensed in the APU, and what are the flight deck indications?

A135: When a fire or overheat is sensed in the APU, the control amplifier triggers the following: FIRE (red) light on the FPP, BTL ARM (amber) light on the FPP, FUEL OPEN (green) advisory light on the FPP goes off, FUEL CLOSED (white) light on the FPP comes on, EXTG (white) switch light on the FPP comes on, CHECK FIRE DET (red) warning light on the Caution and Warning Panel (flashes), Master WARNING PRESS TO RESET (red) light on the Glareshield Panel (flashes), and Fire Bell (optional). The APU will automatically shut down and the FAIL advisory light on the APU control panel will come on.

Q136: How is the APU Fire Protection System tested, and what indications occur during the test?

A136: To test the APU Fire Protection System, the FIRE TEST push button on the Fire Protection Panel (FPP) is pushed. During the test, the control amplifier performs the following: FIRE (red) light on the FPP, BTL ARM (amber) light on the FPP, FUEL OPEN (green) advisory light on the FPP goes off, FUEL CLOSED (white) light on the FPP comes on, EXTG (white) switch light on the FPP comes on, FAULT (amber) light on the FPP comes on, CHECK FIRE DET (red) warning light on the Caution and Warning Panel (flashes), Master WARNING PRESS TO RESET (red) light on the Glareshield Panel (flashes), and Fire Bell (optional). The shutdown signal to the APU FADEC and the APU fuel shut off valve circuits are also tested and the APU shuts down.

Q137: What is the primary function of the Auxiliary Power Unit (APU) Fire Extinguishing System?

A137: The Auxiliary Power Unit (APU) Fire Extinguishing System suppresses fire and overheat conditions in the APU.

Q138: How many fire extinguishing bottles are used for the APU, and how many cartridges does it have?

A138: One fire extinguishing bottle is used to suppress fire and overheat conditions in the APU compartment, and it has one fire extinguisher cartridge.

Q139: Is pilot action required to operate APU fire protection? What automatic actions occur when an APU fire or overheat is sensed?

A139: No pilot action is required to operate APU fire protection. When a fire or overheat is sensed, the control amplifier controls a relay which closes the APU fuel valve. The APU is shut down automatically, and the FAIL advisory light on the APU control panel comes on.

Q140: What happens after a seven-second delay following a fire or overheat detection in the APU? What indications confirm this action?

A140: After a seven-second delay, an electrical signal from the control amplifier ignites an Electro-Explosive Device (EED) in the extinguisher cartridge. The EED explodes, and the fire extinguishing agent is automatically released. The APU BTL ARM light on the FPP goes out, and the BTL LOW light comes on.

Q141: What manual override capability is available if automatic APU fire extinguishing fails?

A141: If automatic fire extinguishing fails and a fire or overheat condition is still sensed, a manual override capability is available by operating the APU EXTG switch located on the FPP.

Q142: Where is the APU fire extinguishing bottle located?

A142: The APU fire extinguishing bottle is located in the aft equipment bay, forward of the APU compartment.

Q143: What type and amount of fire extinguishing agent does the APU fire extinguisher bottle contain, and what is its pressurization?

A143: The APU fire extinguisher is a stainless steel container filled with 4.37 lb. (1.98 kg) of liquid fire extinguishing agent Halon 1301 and pressurized with Nitrogen gas at 600 psig (4136 kPag).

Q144: What is the purpose of the lavatory fire extinguisher?

A144: The lavatory fire extinguisher is used to extinguish a possible fire in the lavatory waste-bin.

Q145: How is the lavatory compartment waste-bin protected from fire? Does it have an electrical interface?

A145: The lavatory compartment waste-bin is protected by a single, thermally-activated fire extinguisher with no electrical interface.

Q146: How does the thermally-activated lavatory fire extinguisher discharge its agent?

A146: When a fire occurs in the lavatory compartment waste-bin, the temperature of the end caps increases. When the temperature reaches a set point, the fusible seals melt and release the end caps, discharging the extinguishing agent into the bin.

Q147: Can the lavatory fire extinguisher be refilled or reused? What periodic maintenance does it require, and what can happen if its discharge tubes are bent?

A147: The lavatory fire extinguisher cannot be refilled or reused. It requires periodic weighing to ensure it is full. If the discharge tubes are bent beyond the specified angle, fire suppression may not work properly.

Q148: What extinguishing agent does the lavatory fire extinguisher use, and what are the specifications of its bottle?

A148: The lavatory fire extinguisher uses bromotrifluoromethane (Halon 1301) as the extinguishing agent. The bottle is a stainless steel sphere with an internal volume of 9 cubic inches (0.15 L), charged with 0.25 pounds (0.113 kilograms) of agent to a pressure of 27 psi (186 kPa).

Q149: At what temperature do the fusible alloy seals melt, releasing the extinguishing agent into the waste-bin?

A149: If the end caps on the discharge tubes reach 174 F (79 C), the fusible alloy seals melt and release the end cap from the tubes, discharging the extinguishing agent.

Q150: Where is the lavatory fire extinguisher located within the aircraft?

A150: The lavatory fire extinguisher is located in the upper amenity assembly, just above the waste-bin. It is attached to the bulkhead with two screws, with the end caps protruding into the waste-bin.
